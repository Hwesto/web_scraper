The relay

Who lands when — average tonnes per month across recent years.

The year opens in the southern-hemisphere summer: Chile and Peru carry January and February. As they fade, Morocco floods spring — its April peak is the single biggest month any origin posts all year. A midsummer lull follows, the one window when British and near-European fruit matters most. Then the baton swings back across the equator: Peru and South Africa ramp hard through the autumn to close the year.

No single country owns the British blueberry. Peru and Morocco run neck-and-neck at the top, together better than half the market; South Africa is the clear third. Chile — the origin this project tracks weekly, fruit by fruit — is smaller by tonnage but lands in the highest-value winter window.

What it costs

Average import price per kilo, by month of the year.

The landed price of an imported kilo hovers around £5.66, but it is not flat. It firms in early autumn — as the southern season restarts and air-freighted early fruit commands a premium — and softens through the high-volume mid-winter and late-spring gluts. Price tracks scarcity, not the calendar.

What every origin charges — and the freight wedge

Two prices per supplier: what the fruit costs leaving its own country (FOB) and what it's worth landed in Britain (CIF). The gap is mostly ocean freight.

Same fruit, two ends of the journey. The grey dot is each origin's own export price; the purple dot is what Britain pays for it at the border (2025). The distance between them is the freight-and-insurance wedge — wide for the deep-sea origins (Chile, South Africa, Argentina ride ~£0.5–1.6/kg of shipping), barely there for short-haul Europe (Spain ≈ £0). Where the wedge goes negative the country is a re-export hub, not a true grower — Dutch and Portuguese "exports" are largely fruit landed elsewhere first, so their FOB sits above the UK border price. A clean tell for which origins actually grow what they ship.

What Chile ships

The varieties inside Chile's punnets, by volume sent to the UK.

Chilean customs records name the cultivar on most shipments, so we can see exactly which berries Britain buys. Legacy dominates — a firm, travel-hardy variety bred for exactly this kind of six-week sea journey — followed by Duke and the premium club varieties Blue Ribbon and Draper.

Where in the world to sell it

Flip the seat. You grow in Chile — where does a kilo net the most?

The same customs data, read the other way, prices every market Chile ships to. After deducting ocean freight, a kilo sent to South Korea nets a grower roughly 28% more than the same kilo into the United States — and the premium survives the longer voyage. Yet the US alone takes 41% of Chilean fruit, because the premium lanes are tiny. Holland looks cheap, but it is a re-export hub — a distribution valve, not a final table.

Who can actually chase the premium

The best price means nothing if your fruit can't get in. China only admits SAG-registered orchards — so which of our named producers are cleared?

Matching our named Chilean exporters against the SAG roster of orchards authorised for China, 23 of 72 producers in our flow are cleared — covering 66% of the UK-bound volume we can name. Those are the growers positioned to steer fruit toward the $1.06/kg premium that Asia nets over the US bulk lane. The rest are locked to the Atlantic markets until they clear registration.

How we know what's coming — two weeks early

Chile's export records publish weeks before Britain's official import figures. By transit-shifting the outbound shipments, we estimate each month's Chilean arrivals about two weeks before HMRC prints them — and the call beats simply assuming "same as last year".
