Britain's blueberry year

Britain eats blueberries every week of the year — yet almost none are grown here in winter. Behind every punnet is a quiet global relay: a baton passed from Peru to Morocco to South Africa to Chile and back, timed so the shelves never go empty. Here is that year, in the data.

The relay

Who lands when — average tonnes per month across recent years.

The year opens in the southern-hemisphere summer: Chile and Peru carry January and February. As they fade, Morocco floods spring — its April peak is the single biggest month any origin posts all year. A midsummer lull follows, the one window when British and near-European fruit matters most. Then the baton swings back across the equator: Peru and South Africa ramp hard through the autumn to close the year.

Who supplies Britain

No single country owns the British blueberry. Peru and Morocco run neck-and-neck at the top, together better than half the market; South Africa is the clear third. Chile — the origin this project tracks weekly, fruit by fruit — is smaller by tonnage but lands in the highest-value winter window.

What it costs

Average import price per kilo, by month of the year.

The landed price of an imported kilo hovers around £5.66, but it is not flat. It firms in early autumn — September and October, as the southern season restarts and air-freighted early fruit commands a premium — and softens through the high-volume mid-winter and late-spring gluts. Price tracks scarcity, not the calendar.

What Chile ships

The varieties inside Chile's punnets, by volume sent to the UK.

Chilean customs records name the cultivar on most shipments, so we can see exactly which berries Britain buys. Legacy dominates — a firm, travel-hardy variety bred for exactly this kind of six-week sea journey — followed by Duke and the premium club varieties Blue Ribbon and Draper. Together they read like a map of what survives the trip and still tastes of something on arrival.

Where in the world to sell it

Now flip the seat. You grow blueberries in Chile — where on Earth does a kilo net you the most?

The same customs data, read the other way, prices every market Chile ships to. After deducting ocean freight, a kilo sent to South Korea nets a Chilean grower roughly 28% more than the same kilo sent to the United States — and the premium survives the longer, dearer voyage. Japan and the smaller Asian markets pay more still. Yet the United States alone takes 41% of all Chilean fruit, because the premium lanes are tiny: Korea and Japan together can't absorb what one week of the US season ships. Holland looks cheap, but it is a re-export hub — a distribution valve, not a final table.

So "where to sell" is never one answer. It is a portfolio: your firmest, certified, earliest fruit chases the Asian premium (where transit and phytosanitary rules let in only the best); the bulk clears through the US; Europe absorbs the rest. The one storm cloud is a 2025 US import tariff that Chilean blueberries were not exempted from — a levy landing squarely on that 40%-of-volume lane.

How we know what's coming — two weeks early

This is the working edge behind the journalism. Chile's export records publish weeks before Britain's official import figures. By transit-shifting the outbound shipments, we estimate each month's Chilean arrivals about two weeks before HMRC prints them — and the call is more accurate than simply assuming "same as last year".
